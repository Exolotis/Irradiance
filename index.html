Section 01
What This Product Is — Plain Language
No jargon. If you own a solar plant, here is what you need to know.

Western India Forging operates a 6,632 kWp solar plant at Bacheri Village, Solapur district. Your plant earns money by converting sunlight into electricity and selling it at ₹9.50 per unit (kWh). The amount of electricity you can produce depends entirely on how much solar energy — technically called GHI (Global Horizontal Irradiance, measured in kWh/m²) — actually falls on your location each year.

In a normal year, Solapur receives about 1,900 kWh/m² of solar irradiance annually. But not every year is normal. In a bad year — heavy clouds, extended monsoon, unusual haze — your plant could receive significantly less sunlight, generating less electricity, earning less revenue. This is a financial risk that is completely outside your control. The sky does what the sky does.

The Core Problem This Insurance Solves

If annual solar irradiance at your plant falls below 1,900 kWh/m² (the Entry Trigger), you start losing revenue relative to expectations. If it falls all the way to 1,400 kWh/m² (the Exit Trigger — 500 kWh/m² below entry), you have lost the maximum insurable amount. This product pays you automatically based on satellite data — no inspection, no argument, no delay.

Why Parametric? Why Not Normal Insurance?

Normal insurance asks: "did something break? Prove it." An adjuster comes, counts the losses, files paperwork — takes months. Your EMIs don't wait months. Parametric insurance is different. It asks a single objective question: "did the satellite-recorded solar irradiance at your location fall below the pre-agreed number?" If yes, you get paid. Automatically. Within 15 days of the phase ending. No forms, no inspector, no negotiation.

Why Solapur's solar profile is distinct from coastal Gujarat, and what the data shows

Bacheri Village, Taluka Malshiras, District Solapur (17.619362°N, 74.921083°E) sits on the interior Deccan Plateau of Maharashtra — a semi-arid, rain-shadow region that receives significantly less monsoon rainfall than coastal areas. This geography makes Solapur one of India's better solar sites by annual irradiance, but it is not immune to monsoon variability. When the Southwest Monsoon is anomalously strong, even this inland location experiences extended cloud cover and reduced irradiance during June–September.

Section 05
AR(1) Residual Model
Solar irradiance has memory — why consecutive bad years cluster, and how we model it

After removing the trend, the remaining year-to-year deviations (residuals) are not purely random. They exhibit autocorrelation — if this year's irradiance was below trend, next year is slightly more likely to also be below trend. The physical driver is ENSO (El Niño–Southern Oscillation): La Niña events typically intensify the Indian monsoon for 1–2 consecutive years, causing back-to-back below-average irradiance in Phase C.

Phase B (Mar–May)+0.19811.42Moderate memory. ENSO El Niño events warm the Deccan plateau pre-monsoon. σ moderate.

Phase C Innovation σ = 38.64 kWh/m² — What This Means

In any given monsoon season, even after accounting for the trend, there is a random shock of ±38.64 kWh/m² in expected GHI. Over 4 months that is ±10 kWh/m² per month — equivalent to roughly 3–4 cloudy days more or less than average each month. This is the fundamental uncertainty that makes monsoon insurance genuinely valuable.

Section 06
Monte Carlo Simulation — 10,000 Paths
How 300,000 simulated GHI values calibrate the triggers with statistical precision

With only 12 post-2013 data points per phase, it is impossible to reliably estimate the 20th percentile from raw data alone (12 observations means P20 is just the 2nd-lowest value — far too sensitive to outliers). Monte Carlo simulation solves this by generating 10,000 plausible futures for each phase, each following the OLS+AR(1) model structure, over a 30-year horizon. The result: 300,000 simulated annual GHI values per phase, from which any percentile can be computed with precision.

1
Generate 10,000 independent paths × 30 years each
Fixed seed = 42. For each path i from 1 to 10,000 and each year t from 1 to 30 (representing 2026–2055), generate a complete time series of phase GHI values.
2
Compute trend component: GHI_trend(t) = α + β × t
Example (Phase C): Trend(2026) = 553.2 + (−3.618) × 13 = 553.2 − 47.0 = 506.2 kWh/m². The trend captures the declining future solar climate.
3
Add AR(1) residual: ε(t) = φ₁ × ε(t−1) + η(t), where η ~ N(0, σ²_η)
This injects realistic year-to-year memory and randomness. Phase C: ε(t) = (−0.042) × ε(t−1) + Normal(0, 38.64²). The innovation σ = 38.64 means large random shocks are common in the monsoon phase.
4
Combine: GHI(t,i) = Trend(t) + ε(t,i)
Each simulated year = deterministic declining trend + random autocorrelated fluctuation. Pool all 300,000 values (30 years × 10,000 paths) and compute the full percentile distribution.

P20 Entry Triggers — All 4 Phases
The exact thresholds that activate insurance payouts and why P20 is the right level

The Entry Trigger is the GHI level below which the insurance begins to pay. It is set at the P20 (20th percentile) of the Monte Carlo simulated distribution — meaning, in any given year, there is a 20% probability of a payout triggering. This is the World Bank / IFC standard for parametric energy insurance: a 1-in-5 year event that is frequent enough to be protective but rare enough for premiums to be affordable.

For the annual product (Cover 1), the entry trigger is 1,900 kWh/m² cumulative annual GHI — this is the P20 of the projected annual distribution. Phase-level triggers are derived proportionally and confirmed by individual phase Monte Carlo simulations.

Section 09
Exit Trigger Scenario Analysis
Three scenarios — Optimistic, Mean, Pessimistic — and the Balanced Exit for each phase

The Exit Trigger is the GHI level at which the insurance pays the maximum possible payout — 100% of that phase's share of the sum insured. Below the exit trigger, no additional payout is made (the cap has been reached). Setting the exit trigger requires judgment: it must be low enough to represent a genuinely severe event, but realistic enough to actually occur within the product's intended design.

The annual exit trigger is fixed contractually at 1,400 kWh/m² — 500 kWh/m² below the 1,900 entry trigger. At the annual level, the payout scales linearly from ₹0 at 1,900 down to the full Sum Insured at 1,400. For each phase, the exit trigger is derived from three scenarios: Optimistic, Mean (Expected), and Pessimistic — then blended using the PERT-weighted Balanced Exit formula.

Balanced Exit = (Optimistic + 4 × Mean + Pessimistic) / 6 This is the PERT (Program Evaluation and Review Technique) formula used in project risk analysis. It weights the Mean (Expected) outcome 4× more than the extreme cases, reflecting that the mean is the most likely outcome, while the extremes bound the range. The formula was originally developed by NASA for schedule uncertainty quantification.

☀️
What happens in this season?
January and February are Solapur's most reliable solar months. The monsoon is long gone, skies are clear, and while the sun is lower in the sky (shorter days), there are almost no clouds. Your plant runs steadily and predictably. Think of this as the "boring but good" season — consistent output, low risk.
⚠️
What could go wrong?
Western disturbances — weather systems that come from the Mediterranean via Pakistan — occasionally bring fog, haze, or thin cloud cover to the Deccan in winter. Dense northern Indian fog can drift southward. Unusual dust storms in February are also possible. These events are rare in Solapur but can cut solar output for days at a time.

Payout = Min( Max( Entry Trigger − Observed Index, 0 ), (Entry Trigger − Exit Trigger) ) × Payout per kWh/m² Where: Entry Trigger = 1,900 kWh/m² (annual cumulative GHI) Exit Trigger = 1,400 kWh/m² (annual cumulative GHI) Observed Index = ERA5-Land annual GHI at 17.6°N 74.9°E Payout per kWh/m² = ₹51,352.19 The Min(...) caps the payout at the Exit Trigger gap (500 kWh/m²). The Max(...,0) ensures no negative payout (no deduction for above-trigger years). Maximum possible payout = 500 × ₹51,352.19 = ₹2,56,76,095 ≈ Sum Insured

ERA5-Land near-real-time reanalysis has approximately a 5-day production lag (data for September 30 is available by October 5). Basis Risk Disclosure

Spatial basis risk: ERA5-Land 11km grid cell averages irradiance across the entire cell. Your plant at a specific corner of the cell may experience 1–3% different irradiance than the cell average, particularly during localised dust events.

Temporal reanalysis error: ERA5-Land accuracy for monthly GHI in Maharashtra: typically ±3–5% for dry months, up to ±8–10% in peak monsoon months (July–August) due to difficulty modelling deep convective cloud systems.

Plant-level basis risk: Panel soiling, inverter downtime, curtailment, and degradation affect actual output but are not reflected in the GHI index. These are not covered by this product — they are the policyholder's operational risk.
