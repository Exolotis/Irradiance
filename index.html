Section 01
What This Project Is
The problem, the solution, and why it is built this way

Imagine you own a solar plant in Surat, Gujarat. You earn money from the electricity your panels produce. That electricity depends entirely on how much sunlight actually hits your panels — technically called GHI (Global Horizontal Irradiance). In a bad monsoon year, or a particularly hazy season, your plant produces less, your income falls, and you cannot pay back loans or cover costs. The risk is real and happens every few years.

Normal insurance does not work well here. A claims inspector has to visit your plant, verify damage, assess loss, file paperwork — it takes months. By then, you have already defaulted. Parametric insurance solves this differently: instead of asking "did you suffer a loss?", it asks a single objective question — "did the measurable solar radiation in your region fall below a pre-agreed threshold?" If yes, you get paid automatically, no inspection needed. The number is the trigger.

Core Logic

If GHI in Phase C (June–September monsoon) falls below 296.60 kWh/m²Phase C P20 TriggerP20_C = 20th percentile of 300,000 MC simulated values = value at sorted position 60,000 = 296.60 kWh/m² Trigger prob: P[GHI_C < 296.60] = 20% = 1-in-5 year eventMonte Carlo: α=492.731, β=−5.403, φ₁=−0.059, σ=15.605, Seed=42, a payout is triggered automatically. No loss assessment. No paperwork. The satellite data says yes or no, and money moves.

Section 02
Raw Data: GHI, ERA5, and the Numbers
Where every number in the Raw Data Reference sheet comes from

What is GHI?

Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) is the total amount of solar energy landing on a flat, horizontal surface per square metre over a given period. It is measured in kWh/m². If GHI for July is 100 kWh/m², it means each square metre received the equivalent of 100 kilowatt-hours of energy from the sun that month.

ERA5-Land Reanalysis

The data source is ERA5-Land Reanalysis produced by ECMWF under the Copernicus Climate Change Service. The specific variable downloaded is SSRD (Surface Solar Radiation Downwards), converted from Joules/m² to kWh/m²/day by dividing by 3,600,000. The dataset covers 1 January 1995 to 31 December 2025 — 31 years, 11,323 daily records.

Annual GHI (1995) = Jan + Feb + ... + Dec = 137.07 + 140.82 + 174.56 + 189.81 + 190.97 + 164.60 + 111.66 + 128.53 + 135.77 + 145.65 + 135.64 + 126.24 = 1781.32 kWh/m²

Why P20 · k-Means · CV=0.018 · Monte Carlo: The Full Science
Mathematical first principles behind every design choice

Why P20 — The Mathematical Argument

For a Normal distribution with mean µ and SD σ, the P20 sits at µ − 0.842σ. This means the trigger fires when GHI is more than 0.84 standard deviations below normal — a well-defined "significantly below average" threshold. The IFC Solar Risk Management framework (2018) and World Bank CCRIF both standardise on P20 as the "moderate stress" parametric energy trigger, based on analysis of 40+ solar insurance products across South Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa.

Normal approx P20 = µ − 0.842 × σ Phase B check: 520.58 − 0.842 × 14.94 = 508.00 Actual simulated P20: 506.99 Error: 1.01 kWh/m² (0.2%) — confirms Normal behaviour ✓ Phase C check: 345.02 − 0.842 × 48.80 = 303.93 Actual simulated P20: 296.60 Error: 7.33 kWh/m² — LEFT-SKEWED distribution! → This is exactly why simulation is essential for Phase C.

What CV = 0.018 Actually Means

CV = Standard Deviation ÷ Mean. For January (CV = 0.018): a "bad" January gives 133.36 − 2.40 = 130.96 kWh/m², a "good" January gives 135.76. The entire swing is 4.8 kWh/m² — barely 3.6%. For July (CV = 0.142): a bad July gives 77.70, a good July gives 118.00. That 40.30 kWh/m² swing represents a 40% range in a single month's revenue. This is the physical source of insurance value.

Payout Model: The Complete Financial Mechanism
Exact formula, rate derivation, and worked examples
Payout per kWp = min( max(Entry − GHI_observed, 0), Entry − Exit ) × Rate Rate = PR × Tariff = 0.80 × ₹2.50 = ₹2.00 per kWh/m² per kWp Example: Phase A, GHI = 275.00 kWh/m²: Shortfall = 276.60 − 275.00 = 1.60 kWh/m² Payout = min(1.60, 4.27) × ₹2.00 = ₹3.20 per kWp Example: Phase A, GHI = 270.00 (below exit — full cap): Shortfall = 276.60 − 270 = 6.60, capped at 4.27 Payout = 4.27 × ₹2.00 = ₹8.54 per kWp (MAXIMUM) Example: Phase A, GHI = 280.00 (above entry — sunny year): max(276.60 − 280, 0) = 0 → ₹0 payout

Section 24 — Explanation Guide
How This Is Calculated: A Complete Walkthrough
1
Data Collection: 31 years ERA5-Land SSRD
Daily SSRD (J/m²) ÷ 3,600,000 = GHI (kWh/m²/day). Summed monthly and annually. 11,323 daily records covering 1 Jan 1995 — 31 Dec 2025.
2
Structural Break Detection: Welch t-test
t = 2.901, p = 0.0078 confirms post-2013 is a statistically distinct lower-solar regime. All calibration uses post-2013 data only (2014–2025).
3
Phase Assignment: k-Means + IMD Seasons
k=4 chosen by elbow method. Results match IMD seasonal classification perfectly — validating physical basis of phases.
4
OLS Trend + AR(1) per Phase
Linear trend fitted on 12 post-2013 observations per phase. AR(1) captures year-to-year memory from ENSO/IOD climate oscillations.
5
Monte Carlo: 10,000 × 30 years (Seed=42)
300,000 forward GHI values per phase. P20 extracted as value at position 60,000. CTE exit = mean of bottom 5%.
6
Wang Transform Pricing + Loading Stack
λ=0.25 market price of risk. Five loadings derived from tail risk measures, IFC cost benchmarks, and volatility-adjusted reserves. GST at 18% per IRDAI mandate.
